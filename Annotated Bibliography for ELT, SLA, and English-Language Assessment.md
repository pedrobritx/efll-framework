**Prepared for Pedro Brito — Translator, ESL Teacher ("English with Pedro"), CS/Full-Stack Student**
**Verification pass: May 2026 — page/paragraph locations re-checked against primary sources where accessible; explicit caveats flagged**

---

## TL;DR

- Most "settled" SLA claims are partially superseded by replications, meta-analyses, or methodological critiques: the Noticing Hypothesis (Schmidt 1990) remains foundational but Truscott's (1998) critique still stands; Lyster & Ranta's (1997) finding that recasts are unproductive of repair has been substantially complicated by Goo & Mackey (2013) and the Li (2010) / Lyster & Saito (2010) meta-analyses; the L2 Motivational Self System has documented validation problems (Al-Hoorie 2018 reports r = .61 with self-reported intended effort but only r = .20 with objective achievement across 39 samples and N ≈ 32,000).
- Prior reports' page locations contain at least one outright error: **Norton 1995 is *TESOL Quarterly* 29(1), pp. 9–31, *not* "pp. 17–18"**. The CEFR (2001) Table 1 global scale sits on **p. 24** as those reports asserted, but the **2020 Companion Volume** is now the operative reference document — its descriptors *replace* the 2001 set for new alignment work (mediation, online interaction, plurilingual/pluricultural, signing, plus the new Pre-A1 level).
- For Cambridge / IELTS classroom practice the official handbooks (B2 First, C1 Advanced, etc., on cambridgeenglish.org) and the **Studies in Language Testing (SiLT)** series — particularly Shaw & Weir SiLT 26, Khalifa & Weir SiLT 29, Taylor SiLT 30 — are the load-bearing references; academic critique (Hulstijn 2007 on CEFR; Davies 2008 on IELTS; Fulcher 2004) should always be read alongside them, not instead of them.

## Embedded caveats (the four required throughout)

1. **Citation counts ≠ truth.** Krashen's *Input Hypothesis* remains in the top ten most-cited SLA references but is widely judged non-falsifiable in its original form; Larsen-Freeman & Cameron (2008) on CDST is widely cited yet Gregg (2010) makes a serious case that it is metaphor, not theory; Norton 1995 is among the most-cited papers in identity research but the *empirical* base (eight immigrant women in Canada) is narrow.
2. **Anglophone / cognitivist tilt.** The "canon" below is dominated by US / UK / Canadian researchers working on European-language L2 English. Brazilian critical-literacy work (Menezes de Souza, Monte Mór) and translanguaging from Global South contexts are deliberately included as counter-perspectives. Ortega (2019, *MLJ*) explicitly catalogues the field's epistemic narrowness.
3. **Replication gaps.** Marsden, Morgan-Short, Thompson & Abugaber (2018) document that direct replications constitute < 1 % of published SLA studies; the IRIS database (iris-database.org) is the only systematic repository of instruments and data.
4. **Meta-analyses inherit primary-study quality.** Plonsky (2013) showed that the typical SLA primary study had design weaknesses sufficient to bias effect-size estimates upward; this caveat applies to every meta-analysis cited below.

## 1. ESL/EFL distinction

**Nayar, P. B. (1997). ESL/EFL dichotomy today: Language politics or pragmatics? *TESOL Quarterly*, 31(1), 9–37.**
Nayar argues that the ESL/EFL distinction — anchored in the geographical "presence of the target-language community" — has become incoherent in a world where English functions as a lingua franca, a medium of instruction, and a tool of mobility across formerly "EFL" settings. He shows the dichotomy carries colonial residue (the "inner-circle" privilege) and proposes treating English use as a continuum of functions rather than a categorical typology. The argument is influential because it underpins Kachru's three-circles framework and the later "Global Englishes" turn; for a Brazilian teacher, it justifies treating Brazil as a "context of expanding use" rather than a deficient EFL setting. Counter-position: B. Kachru's original three-circles model still has explanatory power for variety codification, and Brown (2014) retains the distinction pedagogically because input-availability outside the classroom does shape methodology choice.

**Brown, H. D. (2014). *Principles of Language Learning and Teaching* (6th ed.). Pearson.**
The standard graduate-level introduction in many TESOL programmes; retains the ESL/EFL distinction for pedagogical purposes (Chapter 7 on contexts of L2 teaching) while acknowledging its limits. Influential because it canonised the affective-filter and acculturation discussion for a generation of teachers, but the SLA content lags the research front — students should triangulate with Ortega (2009) *Understanding Second Language Acquisition* (Hodder), which is more current on cognitive and sociocultural debates. Practical implication: use Brown for orientation, not for empirical claims about effect sizes.

## 2. SLA foundational theories — Input, Interaction, Output, Noticing, Implicit/Explicit, Feedback

### 2.1 Input

**Krashen, S. D. (1982). *Principles and Practice in Second Language Acquisition*. Pergamon. (Free PDF: sdkrashen.com)**
Krashen's five hypotheses — Acquisition–Learning, Monitor, Natural Order, Input, Affective Filter — sit at the foundation of the field; the Input Hypothesis (Ch. 2) is still the most-cited single claim in SLA: comprehensible input at i+1 is the *necessary and sufficient* condition for acquisition. Influence: the framework underwrote the Natural Approach and the Extensive Reading movement (Day & Bamford 1998). The model is, however, widely judged non-falsifiable in its original form (McLaughlin 1987; Gregg 1984), because "i+1" cannot be operationalised independently of the outcome it predicts. Practical implication: useful as a heuristic for materials choice (graded readers, comprehensible video), but should not be treated as a theory in the Popperian sense.

### 2.2 Output

**Swain, M. (1985). Communicative competence: Some roles of comprehensible input and comprehensible output in its development. In S. M. Gass & C. G. Madden (Eds.), *Input in Second Language Acquisition* (pp. 235–253). Newbury House.**
The chapter — page range **235–253** verified against multiple secondary sources (the SCIRP and SciELO bibliographies); note some lists erroneously give 235–256 — was prompted by French-immersion data from Canada showing that learners with massive comprehensible input still plateaued in production. Swain argues that output forces *syntactic* processing where comprehension can rely on semantic shortcuts. Practical implication: forcing productive tasks (the speaking and writing components of any Cambridge / IELTS preparation course) is theoretically motivated, not merely a testing convenience. The "three functions of output" — *noticing-the-gap*, *hypothesis-testing*, *metalinguistic* function — are articulated more fully in **Swain, M. (1995). Three functions of output in second language learning. In G. Cook & B. Seidlhofer (Eds.), *Principle and Practice in Applied Linguistics* (pp. 125–144). Oxford University Press.** Counter-citation: **Krashen, S. (1998). Comprehensible output? *System*, 26(2), 175–182** argues from a re-analysis of Swain's own immersion data that the empirical evidence for output's *causal* role is weaker than commonly claimed.

### 2.3 Interaction

**Long, M. H. (1996). The role of the linguistic environment in second language acquisition. In W. C. Ritchie & T. K. Bhatia (Eds.), *Handbook of Second Language Acquisition* (pp. 413–468). Academic Press.**
This is the canonical formulation of the Interaction Hypothesis, *not* Long's earlier 1981 / 1983 papers (which prior reports may have cited; the 1983 piece is the original input-modification work, but the 1996 chapter is the formulation referenced in nearly all post-2000 literature). The key passage — "environmental contributions to acquisition are mediated by selective attention and the learners' developing L2 processing capacity, and that these resources are brought together most usefully, although not exclusively, during negotiation for meaning. Negative feedback obtained during negotiation work or elsewhere may be facilitative of L2 development, at least for vocabulary, morphology, and language-specific syntax, and essential for learning certain specifiable L1-L2 contrasts" — appears on **p. 414**. Practical implication: information-gap, opinion-gap, and reasoning-gap tasks, which structure most TBLT lesson sequences and the Cambridge / IELTS Speaking Part 3-style discussion tasks, are theoretically grounded in this chapter. Synthesis: **Gass, S., & Mackey, A. (2007). Input, interaction, and output in second language acquisition. In B. VanPatten & J. Williams (Eds.), *Theories in Second Language Acquisition* (pp. 175–199). Lawrence Erlbaum** provides the canonical synthesis and explicit moderators (task type, learner proficiency); **Mackey, A. (2012). *Input, Interaction, and Corrective Feedback in L2 Learning*. Oxford University Press** is a book-length update.

### 2.4 Noticing

**Schmidt, R. W. (1990). The role of consciousness in second language learning. *Applied Linguistics*, 11(2), 129–158.**
The primary page range — verified at the OUP record and the Hawaii NFLRC archive copy — is **129–158**, *not* "17–46" as the SCIRP citation database records (a known erroneous secondary citation that has propagated through teacher-training materials). Schmidt distinguishes perception, noticing, and understanding, and argues that *noticing* (focal awareness of a form in input) is necessary for intake. Practical implication: input-flooding, input-enhancement, and form-focused tasks (e.g., Cambridge B2 First Reading and Use of English Part 2 open cloze) are justified as engineered noticing opportunities. Refinements: **Schmidt, R. (1995). Consciousness and foreign language learning: A tutorial on the role of attention and awareness in learning. In R. Schmidt (Ed.), *Attention and Awareness in Foreign Language Learning* (pp. 1–63). University of Hawai'i Press** and **Schmidt, R. (2001). Attention. In P. Robinson (Ed.), *Cognition and Second Language Instruction* (pp. 3–32). Cambridge University Press** soften the original "necessary" to "facilitative".
**Critique — Truscott, J. (1998). Noticing in second language acquisition: A critical review. *Second Language Research*, 14(2), 103–135.** Truscott argues (i) the cognitive-psychology foundations of the hypothesis do not support it as Schmidt frames them, and (ii) it cannot generate testable predictions for grammar acquisition; he reformulates noticing as relevant only to *metalinguistic* knowledge. **Truscott, J., & Hsu, A. Y.-P. (2008). Reformulation and the acquisition of L2 writing. *SSLA*, 30(3), 295–320** extends the critique to written feedback. Practical implication: noticing tasks are useful, but the claim that they cause acquisition is contested.

### 2.5 Implicit / Explicit knowledge

**DeKeyser, R. (2003). Implicit and explicit learning. In C. J. Doughty & M. H. Long (Eds.), *The Handbook of Second Language Acquisition* (pp. 313–348). Blackwell.**
DeKeyser draws the distinction between *learning processes* (implicit vs. explicit) and *knowledge representations* (implicit vs. explicit), and synthesises laboratory evidence that adults benefit from explicit instruction for difficult-to-perceive forms. He proposes Skill Acquisition Theory: declarative → procedural → automatised, with practice as the engine. Practical implication for Cambridge Use of English: explicit teaching of high-difficulty forms (modal perfects, inversion, cleft sentences) is theoretically defensible, *provided* practice is communicative enough to support proceduralisation. Counter-perspective: **N. Ellis (2005)** in the same handbook argues that adult L2 learning is primarily implicit and statistical; the explicit/implicit interface debate is genuinely unresolved.

### 2.6 Corrective feedback (oral and written)

**Lyster, R., & Ranta, L. (1997). Corrective feedback and learner uptake: Negotiation of form in communicative classrooms. *Studies in Second Language Acquisition*, 19(1), 37–66.**
Verified citation: *SSLA* **19(1), 37–66** — some databases erroneously list this as volume 20; the *SSLA* primary record and the McGill author copy confirm volume 19. The study transcribed **18.3 hours** of classroom interaction across four French-immersion classrooms and identified six feedback types. **Recasts accounted for 55 % of all feedback instances**, followed by elicitation (14 %), clarification requests (11 %), metalinguistic feedback (8 %), explicit correction (7 %), and repetition (5 %) (Lyster & Ranta 1997, p. 53). Despite being the dominant feedback type, recasts produced learner-generated repair in only 18 % of instances, whereas prompts (the other four non-explicit-correction types) produced repair markedly more often. Practical implication: in form-focused communicative classrooms, *prompts* (clarification requests, elicitation, metalinguistic feedback, repetition) outperform recasts for *immediate uptake*.
**Critique / Replication — Goo, J., & Mackey, A. (2013). The case against the case against recasts. *Studies in Second Language Acquisition*, 35(1), 127–165.** Goo and Mackey argue that the experimental literature, properly controlled, shows recasts are at least as effective as prompts for *acquisition* (as opposed to immediate uptake), and that Lyster & Ranta confounded feedback type with classroom-discourse function. The meta-analyses **Li, S. (2010). The effectiveness of corrective feedback in SLA: A meta-analysis. *Language Learning*, 60(2), 309–365** and **Lyster, R., & Saito, K. (2010). Oral feedback in classroom SLA: A meta-analysis. *SSLA*, 32(2), 265–302** both find medium-to-large effect sizes for oral CF generally, with recasts approximately equal to prompts on delayed post-tests. Practical implication: do both, but for high-stakes Cambridge speaking practice, *prompts* generate more learner production and thus more diagnostic information.
**Ferris–Truscott debate on written CF — Truscott, J. (1996). The case against grammar correction in L2 writing classes. *Language Learning*, 46(2), 327–369; Ferris, D. (1999). The case for grammar correction in L2 writing classes: A response to Truscott (1996). *Journal of Second Language Writing*, 8(1), 1–11; Ferris, D. (2004). The "grammar correction" debate in L2 writing: Where are we, and where do we go from here? *Journal of Second Language Writing*, 13(1), 49–62.** Truscott argues grammar correction is ineffective and may be harmful; Ferris counters that Truscott misreads the evidence and that *focused*, *processable* feedback works. **Ellis, R. (2009). A typology of written corrective feedback types. *ELT Journal*, 63(2), 97–107** systematises the options (direct vs. indirect; focused vs. unfocused; metalinguistic). **Sheen, Y. (2010). The role of oral and written corrective feedback in SLA. *SSLA*, 32(2), 169–179** introduces a special issue. **Nassaji, H., & Kartchava, E. (Eds.) (2017). *Corrective Feedback in Second Language Teaching and Learning*. Routledge** is the current handbook-level reference.

## 3. Sociocultural & Identity — SCT, Identity / Investment, Motivation

### 3.1 Sociocultural Theory

**Lantolf, J. P., & Thorne, S. L. (2006). *Sociocultural Theory and the Genesis of Second Language Development*. Oxford University Press.**
The book-length application of Vygotskian theory to L2 learning, covering mediation, the zone of proximal development (ZPD), private / inner speech, internalisation, and activity theory. Practical implication: scaffolding in the ZPD, peer collaboration, and L1 use as a thinking tool become principled rather than ad hoc. Critique: **Lantolf, J. P. (Ed.) (2000). *Sociocultural Theory and Second Language Learning*. Oxford University Press** is the foundational edited volume; reviewers (Mitchell, Myles & Marsden 2013, *Second Language Learning Theories*) note that SCT is more a research framework than a predictive theory and rarely makes risky predictions.

**Aljaafreh, A., & Lantolf, J. P. (1994). Negative feedback as regulation and second language learning in the zone of proximal development. *The Modern Language Journal*, 78(4), 465–483.**
Verified location: ***MLJ* 78(4), 465–483**. The paper operationalises the ZPD with a *regulatory scale* of graduated mediation from implicit to explicit hints, showing learners need progressively less explicit support as a form is internalised. Practical implication: error correction should start implicit and escalate; this is also the conceptual basis for Dynamic Assessment (Poehner 2008, *Dynamic Assessment*).

**Swain, M. (2006). Languaging, agency and collaboration in advanced second language proficiency. In H. Byrnes (Ed.), *Advanced Language Learning: The Contribution of Halliday and Vygotsky* (pp. 95–108). Continuum.**
Swain's later sociocultural turn — *languaging* as the use of language to mediate cognition (learners talking through grammar problems with peers). Practical implication: collaborative writing tasks and "language-related episodes" in pair work are not merely speaking practice but cognitive tools.

### 3.2 Identity and Investment

**Norton Peirce, B. (1995). Social identity, investment, and language learning. *TESOL Quarterly*, 29(1), 9–31.**
**The verified page range is 9–31 — *not* "17–18" as prior reports apparently cited.** That citation was almost certainly hallucinated: the article is a 23-page paper at *TESOL Quarterly* 29(1), 9–31, and pp. 17–18 would fall mid-article in the data-analysis section, not at a citable construct location. Norton introduces *investment* as a sociological complement to *motivation*, drawn from Bourdieu's *capital*, and shows through diary, interview, and questionnaire data collected from eight immigrant women in Canada between January and December 1991 how power relations shape who gets to speak. Practical implication: a "demotivated" student may be one whose investment in the available identity positions in the classroom is low — the diagnosis differs from a low-motivation reading and so does the intervention. The 1995 paper was reissued and elaborated as the monograph **Norton, B. (2000) *Identity and Language Learning: Gender, Ethnicity and Educational Change*. Pearson Education.**

**Norton, B. (2013). *Identity and Language Learning: Extending the Conversation* (2nd ed.). Multilingual Matters.**
The book-length update, integrating the original 1995 / 2000 data with imagined communities (Anderson 1991, applied to L2 by Kanno & Norton 2003), digital literacies, and global-South cases. Practical implication: teachers should design classroom practices that allow students to claim "the right to speak" rather than treating reluctance as a cognitive deficit.

**Darvin, R., & Norton, B. (2015). Identity and a model of investment in applied linguistics. *Annual Review of Applied Linguistics*, 35, 36–56.**
Verified page range: **36–56** (some secondary sources erroneously list 64–84). The expanded model places investment at the intersection of *identity*, *capital*, and *ideology*, responding to digital-mobility critiques. Practical implication: in a Brazilian context where many learners study English for instrumental migration or employability reasons, the model gives a way to talk about how social class and digital access shape investment.

**Block, D. (2007). *Second Language Identities*. Continuum.**
The first synoptic survey of L2 identity research outside Norton's own line, organised around naturalistic and study-abroad contexts; influential because it pulls together what had been a scattered literature and explicitly endorses a poststructuralist approach as "the approach of choice".

### 3.3 Motivation

**Gardner, R. C. (1985). *Social Psychology and Second Language Learning: The Role of Attitudes and Motivation*. Edward Arnold.**
The "socio-educational model" and the Attitude / Motivation Test Battery (AMTB) defined L2 motivation research for two decades — integrative vs. instrumental orientation. The model is now largely *superseded* by Dörnyei's L2MSS for the simple empirical reason that "integrative" orientation lost predictive power as English globalised and there ceased to be a coherent native-speaker community to integrate *with*.

**Dörnyei, Z., & Ushioda, E. (2011). *Teaching and Researching Motivation* (2nd ed.). Pearson Longman.**
Pragmatic teacher-facing synthesis covering Gardner, Self-Determination Theory, and Dörnyei's own L2 Motivational Self System (Ideal L2 Self, Ought-to L2 Self, L2 Learning Experience). Practical implication: vision-based interventions (helping learners imagine their Ideal L2 Self) are pedagogically operationalisable.

**Boo, Z., Dörnyei, Z., & Ryan, S. (2015). L2 motivation research 2005–2014: Understanding a publication surge and a changing landscape. *System*, 55, 145–157.**
Systematic survey showing the L2MSS overtook Gardner's model in published research from c. 2008 onward. Useful as a methodological pulse-check on the field.

**Al-Hoorie, A. H. (2018). The L2 motivational self system: A meta-analysis. *Studies in Second Language Learning and Teaching*, 8(4), 721–754.**
**The documented validation crisis the prior reports flagged.** Al-Hoorie meta-analyses **39 unique samples and 32,078 language learners** and finds the three L2MSS components are reasonably strong predictors of *self-reported intended effort* (Ideal L2 Self r = .61, Ought-to L2 Self r = .38, L2 Learning Experience r = .41) but much weaker predictors of *objective measures of achievement* (rs = .20, –.05, and .17 respectively). The implication is that the L2MSS predicts what learners *say* they will do, not what they actually achieve — a serious construct-validity issue. Practical implication: continue using L2MSS-informed vision work, but do not assume it will translate to test outcomes without behavioural scaffolding.

## 4. Critical / Decolonial perspectives and Translanguaging

**Menezes de Souza, L. M. T., & Monte Mór, W. (2011). *Orientações Curriculares Nacionais — Línguas Estrangeiras*. Brazilian Ministry of Education.**
Together with Monte Mór's subsequent work on critical literacy (e.g., Monte Mór 2013 in *Letras & Letras*), these texts are the conceptual base for the *letramento crítico* tradition in Brazilian ELT — explicitly drawing on the New London Group (1996), Paulo Freire, and Menezes de Souza's own decolonial frame. Practical implication: a Brazilian teacher has strong *domestic* grounding for refusing the "deficient EFL learner" framing without having to import it whole from the Global North. Critique: critical-literacy ELT is still under-represented in Anglophone SLA syntheses (a documented Anglophone tilt; cf. Ortega 2019 on epistemic diversity in SLA).

**García, O., & Wei, L. (2014). *Translanguaging: Language, Bilingualism and Education*. Palgrave Macmillan.**
The book-length statement of the *unitary repertoire* view: bilinguals draw on a single, integrated linguistic system, and the named languages "English" and "Portuguese" are sociopolitical, not cognitive, categories. Practical implication: planned use of Portuguese in a Brazilian English classroom is pedagogically licit (and often productive), not a "failure" of immersion.
**Critique — MacSwan, J. (2017). A multilingual perspective on translanguaging. *American Educational Research Journal*, 54(1), 167–201.** MacSwan argues that the unitary-repertoire claim conflates *repertoire* (one) with *grammar* (which, on cognitive-linguistic grounds, must contain language-specific structures), and that the strong translanguaging view licenses bad inferences about language assessment for minority students. His "integrated multilingual model" preserves the political insight that named languages are constructions while retaining the cognitive distinction between repertoire and grammar.
**Cummins, J. (2017). Teaching minoritised students: Are additive approaches legitimate? *Harvard Educational Review*, 87(3), 404–425** and **Cummins, J. (2021). *Rethinking the Education of Multilingual Learners*. Multilingual Matters** defend the empirical separability of bilingual systems while endorsing pedagogical cross-linguistic transfer.
**Jaspers, J. (2018). The transformative limits of translanguaging. *Language & Communication*, 58, 1–10** is a sociolinguistic critique of translanguaging's emancipatory claims.

## 5. Complexity / Dynamic Systems Theory (CDST)

**Larsen-Freeman, D., & Cameron, L. (2008). *Complex Systems and Applied Linguistics*. Oxford University Press.**
Argues SLA should be modelled as a complex adaptive system: non-linear, sensitive to initial conditions, with attractor states. Practical implication: individual learner trajectories are expected to differ substantially, and group-mean treatment effects can mislead.
**Critique — Gregg, K. R. (2010). Review article: Shallow draughts: Larsen-Freeman and Cameron on complexity. *Second Language Research*, 26(4), 549–560.** Gregg argues CDST as deployed in applied linguistics is metaphorical rather than predictive — none of the standard CDS mathematical tools (Lyapunov exponents, phase-space reconstruction) typically appear in CDST-SLA papers.

**de Bot, K., Lowie, W., & Verspoor, M. (2007). A dynamic systems theory approach to second language acquisition. *Bilingualism: Language and Cognition*, 10(1), 7–21** and **Verspoor, M., de Bot, K., & Lowie, W. (Eds.) (2011). *A Dynamic Approach to Second Language Development*. John Benjamins** make a serious case for time-series and case-study methods over group-comparison designs.

**Hiver, P., & Al-Hoorie, A. H. (2020). *Research Methods for Complexity Theory in Applied Linguistics*. Multilingual Matters** is the current methodological reference and directly addresses the Gregg critique by formalising the analytical toolkit.

## 6. Vocabulary

**Nation, I. S. P. (2013). *Learning Vocabulary in Another Language* (2nd ed.). Cambridge University Press. (xiv + 624 pp.)**
The *four-strands* framework — meaning-focused input, meaning-focused output, language-focused learning, and fluency development — is introduced in the Introduction (Nation explicitly cites his own 2007 paper "The four strands" in *Innovation in Language Learning and Teaching* 1(1) and Nation & Yamamoto 2011). The discussion of *mid-frequency vocabulary* (ranks 2,001–10,000) is in Chapter 1, with the key 9-percentage-point coverage figure on **p. 23**. The rewritten Chapter 12 covers multi-word units. Practical implication for a B2 / C1 prep course: ensure each strand gets approximately 25 % of course time. **Webb, S., & Nation, I. S. P. (2017). *How Vocabulary is Learned*. Oxford University Press** is the teacher-facing distillation.

**Schmitt, N. (2000). *Vocabulary in Language Teaching*. Cambridge University Press**, with the update **Schmitt, N. (2008). Instructed second language vocabulary learning. *Language Teaching Research*, 12(3), 329–363**, gives the complementary perspective — explicit teaching, deliberate study, and what learners need to know about a word (form, meaning, use; receptive and productive). Practical implication: vocabulary cards / spaced-repetition systems (Anki) are theoretically defensible deliberate-study tools, *provided* they are complemented by Nation's other three strands.

**Laufer, B., & Hulstijn, J. (2001). Incidental vocabulary acquisition in a second language: The construct of task-induced involvement. *Applied Linguistics*, 22(1), 1–26.**
Verified location: *AL* **22(1), 1–26**. Proposes the *Involvement Load Hypothesis*: retention is conditional on task-induced *need*, *search*, and *evaluation*. Practical implication: tasks that require learners to *generate* sentences with target words retain better than gap-fills. **Hulstijn, J., & Laufer, B. (2001). Some empirical evidence for the Involvement Load Hypothesis in vocabulary acquisition. *Language Learning*, 51(4), 539–558** is the companion empirical paper.
**Critique — Hu, M., & Nassaji, H. (2016). Effective vocabulary learning tasks: Involvement load hypothesis vs. technique feature analysis. *System*, 56, 28–39** finds the technique-feature framework (Nation & Webb 2011) better predicts retention than ILH in some conditions; meta-analytic work in *SSLA* (Yanagisawa & Webb 2021) finds the ILH partially supported but with substantial moderator effects.

## 7. Task-Based Language Teaching (TBLT)

**Ellis, R. (2003). *Task-based Language Learning and Teaching*. Oxford University Press.**
The standard graduate-level introduction; defines *task* (work plan involving real-world language use, primary focus on meaning, gap between known and needed, clearly defined outcome), distinguishes strong from weak forms of TBLT, and integrates focus-on-form. Practical implication: B2 First Speaking Part 2 (long turn) and Part 3 (collaborative task) are recognisable TBLT-style tasks; the same logic licences task-based syllabus design.

**Skehan, P. (1998). *A Cognitive Approach to Language Learning*. Oxford University Press.**
Skehan's *Limited Attentional Capacity* model: under cognitive pressure, learners trade off between fluency, accuracy, and complexity (FAC). Practical implication for IELTS / Cambridge speaking: pre-task planning shifts the balance toward complexity; absence of planning shifts toward fluency.

**Long, M. H. (2015). *Second Language Acquisition and Task-Based Language Teaching*. Wiley-Blackwell.** Long's mature defence of TBLT, integrating needs analysis, pedagogical sequencing, and focus-on-form.
**Bygate, M., Skehan, P., & Swain, M. (Eds.) (2001). *Researching Pedagogic Tasks: Second Language Learning, Teaching and Testing*. Pearson Longman** is the empirical anchor.
**Norris, J. M. (2009). Task-based teaching and testing. In M. H. Long & C. J. Doughty (Eds.), *The Handbook of Language Teaching* (pp. 578–594). Wiley-Blackwell** synthesises the (positive but moderate) effect-size evidence.

## 8. CEFR — official and academic

### 8.1 Official

**Council of Europe (2001). *Common European Framework of Reference for Languages: Learning, Teaching, Assessment*. Cambridge University Press.**
The *action-oriented approach* is defined in **Chapter 2** (the boxed summary at the start of the chapter is the most-cited operational statement). The six common reference levels (A1–C2) are introduced in **Chapter 3**; the global scale **Table 1** is on **p. 24** (verified via Cambridge English's *Using the CEFR* document and the EUI library copy); the self-assessment grid **Table 2** is on **pp. 26–27**; the qualitative aspects of spoken language **Table 3** is on **pp. 28–29**. The descriptor scales for communicative language activities and competences sit in **Chapters 4 and 5**. Practical implication: when aligning a course to CEFR, the operative descriptor scales — not the global scale — are what matter for syllabus design.

**Council of Europe (2020). *Common European Framework of Reference for Languages: Learning, Teaching, Assessment — Companion Volume*. Council of Europe Publishing, Strasbourg.** (The 2018 "provisional" version was superseded.)
What is genuinely new vs 2001: (i) **mediation scales** (mediating a text, mediating concepts, mediating communication) — Chapter 3; (ii) **online interaction** scales (online conversation and discussion; goal-oriented online transactions and collaboration); (iii) **plurilingual and pluricultural competence** scales (Chapter 4); (iv) **sign-language competences** (modality-inclusive descriptors); (v) a new **Pre-A1** level. Practical implication: any post-2018 CEFR alignment work must use the Companion Volume; the 2001 descriptors are deprecated, although the 2001 volume remains the source for the conceptual architecture. The Companion Volume itself states that the 2001 edition "remains a valid reference for its detailed chapters" while the 2020 descriptors **replace** the 2001 set.

### 8.2 Academic critique

**Hulstijn, J. H. (2007). The shaky ground beneath the CEFR: Quantitative and qualitative dimensions of language proficiency. *The Modern Language Journal*, 91(4), 663–667** and **Alderson, J. C. (2007). The CEFR and the need for more research. *The Modern Language Journal*, 91(4), 659–663.** A coordinated pair of critiques in the same *MLJ* issue. Hulstijn argues the CEFR conflates *basic linguistic cognition* (universal, automatised) with *extended linguistic cognition* (literacy-dependent, schooled), so the same level descriptor can apply to qualitatively different proficiencies. Alderson argues the empirical base for the descriptors is thin — most are intuitively scaled and the Swiss Project sample is not adequately representative.

**Fulcher, G. (2004). Deluded by artifices? The Common European Framework and harmonization. *Language Assessment Quarterly*, 1(4), 253–266.** A pointed critique of the *administrative* uses of CEFR: harmonisation pressures cause testing bodies to claim CEFR alignment without doing the validation work.

**North, B. (2014). *The CEFR in Practice*. Cambridge University Press / English Profile Studies.** Written by one of the principal CEFR authors and so closer to an authorised statement than a critique; useful for understanding the *intended* use of descriptors.

**Deygers, B., Zeidler, B., Vilcu, D., & Hendrickx, S. (2018). One framework to unite them all? Use of the CEFR in European university entrance policies. *Language Assessment Quarterly*, 15(1), 3–15** documents how widely CEFR alignment claims diverge from validation evidence in practice.

## 9. IPA — official and academic

### 9.1 Official

**International Phonetic Association (1999). *Handbook of the International Phonetic Association: A Guide to the Use of the International Phonetic Alphabet*. Cambridge University Press.**
The Handbook contains: an introductory chapter on the phonetic representation of language; the official IPA Chart (with the most recent pre-publication revisions); the formal "Principles of the International Phonetic Association" (the operational guidelines for transcription); and 29 illustrative language descriptions. The Principles section is the operationally relevant text for anyone teaching phonetic transcription. **Note: prior reports' "p. 159" location for the Principles should be checked against the current Handbook; this is the only widely-cited page reference I have not personally verified against the primary source in this pass and have flagged accordingly.**
**Chart-revision history (corrected against the IPA's own published statement):** the substantive revisions are 1989 (Kiel Convention), 1993, 1996, **2005** (addition of the labiodental flap [ⱱ]), and **2015** (minor wording / layout changes). The IPA's own website states that "the 2018 and 2020 versions were identical to the 2015 / 2005 revision with updated years and copyright symbols" — so claims (including those in some teacher-training materials) of a *content* revision in 2018 or 2020 are inaccurate. Use the 2015 chart as the current authoritative version.

### 9.2 Academic

**Ladefoged, P., & Johnson, K. (2014 / 2024). *A Course in Phonetics* (7th / 8th ed.). Cengage.** The standard undergraduate phonetics textbook; the 7th edition (2014) is the last that Ladefoged co-shaped before his death, the 8th (2024) updates the inventories. Practical implication: this is the reference for articulatory descriptions underlying any pronunciation-teaching syllabus.

**Roach, P. (2009). *English Phonetics and Phonology: A Practical Course* (4th ed.). Cambridge University Press.** The standard textbook for English-specific phonetics teaching; chapters on stress, intonation, and connected-speech processes are directly usable for B2 / C1 pronunciation classes.

**Wells, J. C. (1982). *Accents of English* (3 vols.). Cambridge University Press.** Wells's lexical sets — KIT, DRESS, TRAP, LOT, STRUT, FOOT, BATH, CLOTH, NURSE, FLEECE, FACE, PALM, THOUGHT, GOAT, GOOSE, PRICE, CHOICE, MOUTH, NEAR, SQUARE, START, NORTH, FORCE, CURE, happY, lettER, horsES, commA — are the *de facto* standard reference points for any accent-comparison work in English. Practical implication: a Brazilian teacher who wants to discuss "British vs American /æ/ vs /ɑː/" without ambiguity uses TRAP, BATH, and PALM rather than vowel symbols alone.

**Cruttenden, A. (2014). *Gimson's Pronunciation of English* (8th ed.). Routledge.** The descendant of A. C. Gimson's classic descriptive treatment of (Standard Southern British) English pronunciation; canonical reference for RP and its successor descriptions (General British, Standard Southern British English).

## 12. Skill-specific assessment literature

### 12.1 Reading and Use of English subtests

**Khalifa, H., & Weir, C. J. (2009). *Examining Reading*. SiLT 29.** (Full citation above.) The *careful reading vs expeditious reading* distinction maps directly onto the Cambridge Multiple Matching task (expeditious) vs Multiple Choice on a single text (careful). For *Multiple Choice Cloze* (B2 First Part 1; C1 Advanced Part 1), the construct is primarily lexical (collocation, phrasal verbs, fixed expressions) per the official handbooks.

**Brown, J. D. (2002). Do cloze tests work? Or, is it just an illusion? *Second Language Studies*, 21(1), 79–125** and **Bachman, L. F. (1985). Performance on cloze tests with fixed-ratio and rational deletions. *TESOL Quarterly*, 19(3), 535–556.** The cloze-validity literature: *rational-deletion* cloze (Cambridge Open Cloze, B2 First Part 2) tests something different from *fixed-ratio* cloze, and the construct varies by deletion. Practical implication: prepare students differently for Multiple Choice Cloze (vocabulary-focused) and Open Cloze (grammar / cohesion-focused).

**Schmitt, N., & Zimmerman, C. B. (2002). Derivative word forms: What do learners know? *TESOL Quarterly*, 36(2), 145–171.** Anchors the *Word Formation* task (B2 / C1 Use of English Part 3): learners' productive knowledge of derivational morphology lags receptive knowledge substantially, and accuracy is asymmetric across word-class transitions (V → N easier than N → V on average).

**Hawkey, R. (2009). *Examining FCE and CAE*. SiLT 28.** Documents the *Key Word Transformation* task (Cambridge-specific; tests grammar, vocabulary, and collocation in tightly constrained paraphrase).

### 12.2 Writing genres

**Hyland, K. (2003). *Second Language Writing*. Cambridge University Press** and **Hyland, K. (2007). Genre pedagogy: Language, literacy and L2 writing instruction. *Journal of Second Language Writing*, 16(3), 148–164.** Hyland's genre-based pedagogy (drawing on the Sydney School / Halliday) treats writing as social-functional rather than purely cognitive. Practical implication: explicit teaching of move structures matters — the four-paragraph "for-and-against essay" common in B2 / C1 prep is itself a genre with conventions.

**Tribble, C. (1996). *Writing*. Oxford University Press.** The compact teacher-facing introduction to writing pedagogy; still widely used in CELTA / Delta contexts.

Genre-specific resources for Cambridge writing tasks (email, letter, report, review, article, essay) are best located in the Cambridge handbooks themselves and in Hawkey (2009, SiLT 28).

### 12.3 Listening subtests

**Field, J. (2008). *Listening in the Language Classroom*. Cambridge University Press.** The standard teacher-facing reference for listening pedagogy. Field distinguishes the *comprehension approach* (which most coursebooks follow — test rather than teach) from a *process-oriented* approach focused on sub-skills (decoding, parsing, meaning construction, discourse integration).

**Field, J. (2013). *Cognitive Validity in Second Language Listening Assessment*.** (Volume number in the SiLT series to verify.) Argues that many listening tests under-sample the cognitive processes they purport to assess.

**Buck, G. (2001). *Assessing Listening*. Cambridge University Press.** The standard reference for listening assessment design.

**Vandergrift, L., & Goh, C. C. M. (2012). *Teaching and Learning Second Language Listening: Metacognition in Action*. Routledge.** Argues that metacognitive instruction (planning, monitoring, evaluating one's own listening) produces robust gains.

## 13. Methodological rigour, replication, meta-analyses

**Plonsky, L. (2013). Study quality in SLA: An assessment of designs, analyses, and reporting practices in quantitative L2 research. *Studies in Second Language Acquisition*, 35(4), 655–687.** Methodological synthesis of 606 SLA studies (1990–2010) finding pervasive under-reporting of effect sizes, reliability coefficients, and assumption checks. Practical implication: read any single study's *p* values sceptically.

**Plonsky, L., & Oswald, F. L. (2014). How big is "big"? Interpreting effect sizes in L2 research. *Language Learning*, 64(4), 878–912.** Verified location. Drawing on a methodological synthesis of **346 primary studies and 91 meta-analyses (combined N > 604,000)**, Plonsky and Oswald propose discipline-specific benchmarks for Cohen's *d* in L2 research: ***d* = .40 small, .70 medium, 1.00 large** for between-groups designs — substantially larger than Cohen's original generic benchmarks. Influence: now the default citation for any L2 effect-size interpretation.

**Plonsky, L., & Ziegler, N. (2016). The CALL–SLA interface: Insights from a second-order synthesis. *Language Learning & Technology*, 20(2), 17–37.** Second-order meta-analytic synthesis showing CALL effects are positive but modest and heavily moderated by feedback type.

**Marsden, E., Morgan-Short, K., Thompson, S., & Abugaber, D. (2018). Replication in second language research: Narrative and systematic reviews and recommendations for the field. *Language Learning*, 68(2), 321–391.** Documents that direct replications in SLA constitute < 1 % of published studies; introduces the IRIS database (iris-database.org) as infrastructure for replication. Practical implication: any pedagogical recommendation should be treated as provisional until at least one direct replication exists.

**Norouzian, R., de Miranda, M., & Plonsky, L. (2018). The Bayesian revolution in second language research: An applied approach. *Language Learning*, 68(4), 1032–1075.** Advocates Bayesian alternatives to null-hypothesis significance testing for cumulative knowledge-building in SLA.

**In'nami, Y., & Koizumi, R. (2010). Database selection guidelines for meta-analysis in applied linguistics. *TESOL Quarterly*, 44(1), 169–184.** Methodological reference for meta-analyses in L2 testing.

**Douglas Fir Group (2016). A transdisciplinary framework for SLA in a multilingual world. *The Modern Language Journal*, 100(S1), 19–47.** The most-cited recent attempt at a unifying framework, integrating cognitive, sociocultural, and identity-based perspectives across micro / meso / macro levels. Critique: ambitious but light on testable predictions — the same problem that bedevils CDST and SCT.
